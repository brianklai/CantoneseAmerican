# Cantonese American Editorial Guide

## Project voice

- Culturally serious, internet-native.
- Commentary first, clip second.
- Cantonese and Chinese-American framing should stay visible.
- Transparent uncertainty is better than fake authority.
- Each scene should answer one question: why does this belong in the archive?

## What we are making

Cantonese American is not a fandom wiki and not a neutral database. Each entry should feel like a cultural filing note written by someone who understands that a scene can matter because of language, cadence, setting, migration history, neighborhood texture, or memory.

## House rules

- Lead with the scene's social meaning, not trivia.
- Treat Cantonese as lived American language, not exotic garnish.
- If the dialect is unclear, say so plainly.
- If a memory is community-submitted, label it as such until sourced.
- Keep the archive voice sharp, readable, and specific. Do not drift into academic fog or generic SEO copy.
- Do not invent transcripts, episode numbers, or quotes to make a draft feel complete.

## What makes a strong scene

- A clip where Cantonese changes how the American scene reads.
- A moment that documents neighborhood, business, family, politics, or media texture.
- A scene with enough evidence to support commentary, translation, and citation.
- A scene that would still matter if the clip had to come down tomorrow.

## Entry shape

- `title`: memorable, magazine-like, searchable.
- `subtitle`: one-sentence deck that frames the scene.
- `description`: what happens and why it matters.
- `commentary`: the interpretive layer.
- `culturalSignificance`: why the scene belongs in the record.
- `accuracyNotes` and `verificationNotes`: what is still provisional.

## Tone checks before publish

- Does the entry sound like a real editor wrote it?
- Does it explain the scene without flattening it into a novelty clip?
- Does it make uncertainty legible where needed?
- Does it preserve dignity for the people on screen and the community being described?
